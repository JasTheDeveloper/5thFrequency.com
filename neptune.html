Neptune

Neptune is the eighth farthest planet from the Sun. Like Uranus, Neptune is classified as an "ice giant," meaning its composition includes heavier elements such as water, ammonia, and methane alongside hydrogen and helium. These "ices" are not solid but exist in fluid or gaseous states under Neptune's extreme conditions. Neptune has a striking deep blue appearance, which is primarily due to the presence of methane in its atmosphere. Methane absorbs red light and reflects blue light, but the exact reason for Neptune's vivid blue color is not fully understood and may involve additional atmospheric components. Neptune's atmosphere is composed mainly of hydrogen (80%) and helium (19%), with traces of methane and other compounds. The planet has visible cloud bands, storms, and high-altitude clouds made of frozen methane. Neptune has the most violent weather in the solar system, with the fastest winds recorded at up to 1,300 miles per hour. The planet experiences large storm systems, including the "Great Dark Spot," a massive storm to Jupiter's Great Red Spot, though it appeared and disappeared overtime. Despite it's distance from the Sun, Neptune radiates more heat than it receives, suggesting an interanal heat source. However, the average temperature in Neptune's atmosphere is extremely cold, around -373 degrees fahrenheit. Neptune rotates relatively quickly, completing one rotation (a day) in about 16 hours. Neptune's magnetic field, like Uranus', is unusually tilted, about 47 degrees from its rotational axis, and it is offset from the planet's center. This creates a complex and dynamic magnetic environment. Neptune has a faint ring system consisting of five main rings made up of dust and small particles. These rings are much darker and less pronounced than Saturn's, and they are thought to be relatively yound and short-lived. Neptune has 14 known moons, the largest of which is Triton.
