Moon

The Moon, Earth's only natural satellite, has fascinated humans for centuries. It plays a crucial role in influencing our planet and is the most studied celestial body after Earth. The Moon is roughly one-quarter the size of Earth. The Moon goes through eight distinct phases over the course of its roughly 29.5 day cycle, known as the lunar month. The phases are caused by the changing angles between the Earth, Moon, and Sun as the Moon orbits the Earth. The Moon is tidally locked to Earth, meaning the same side of the Moon always faces Earth. This happens because the Moons's rotation period is the same as its orbital period around Earth. The side of the Moon we never see from Earth is called the far side or sometimes mistakenly the "dark side" (though both sides receive sunlight). This far side remained unseen until 1959, when it was photographed by the Soviet Luna 3 spacecraft. The Moon's gravity is about 1/6th of Earth's gravity. This means that a person weighing 180 pounds on Earth would weigh only 30 pounds on the Moon. This lower gravity contributes to the Moon's lack of substantial atmosphere, as the gravity is not strong enough to hold onto gases for a long time. The Moon has an extremely thin atmosphere called an exosphere, which is composed mostly of hydrogen, helium, neon, and other trace gases. It's so thin that it cannot support weather or breathable air. The Moon is covered in impact craters from meteoroids, comets, and asteroids. Because there is no atmosphere to protect the Moon, impacts remain visible for millions of years. Temperatures on the Moon vary drastically due to its lack of atmosphere. During the lunar day, temperatures can reach 260 degrees Fahrenheit, while at night they can plummet to -280 degrees Fahrenheit. Recent discoveries have confirmed the presence of water ice in permanently shadowed craters at the lunar poles. There are also traces of water within the lunar soil, bound to minerals. This has significant implications for future lunar exploration and potential colonization. The most widely accepted theory about the Moon's formaion is the Giant Impact Hypothesis. This theory suggests that the Moon was formed around 4.5 billion years ago from debris resulting from a collision between Earth and a Mars-sized body call Theia. The Moon's gravitational pull on Earth is the primary driver of ocean tides. The gravitational interaction between the Earth and the Moon causes the rise and fall of sea levels. The Moon helps stabilize Earth's axial tilt, which in turn regulates the seasons and contributes to the stability of Earth's climate over long periods. Unlike Earth, the Moon does not have a global magnetic field. However, certain areas of the lunar surface are magnetized, likely remnants of an ancient magnetic field. A lunar eclpise occurs when the Earth comes directly between the Sun and the Moon, casting a shadow on the Moon. Depending on the alignment, the Moon can take on a reddish hue during a total lunar eclipse, often referred to as a "blood moon."
