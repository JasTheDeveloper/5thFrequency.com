Jupiter

Jupiter, the largest planet is our solar system, is a gas giant. It has more than 300 times the mass of Earth and could fit about 1,300 Earths inside it. Jupiter is primarily composed of hydrogen (about 90%) and helium (about 10%), similar to the Sun. It has no solid surface; instead, it is a massive ball of gas, with a likely small rocky core deep within. One of Jupiter's most famous features is the Great Red Spot, a giant storm larger than Earth that has been raging for at least 350 years. The storm has winds reaching speeds of over 400mph. Jupiter has a powerful magnetic field, about 14 times stronger than Earth's. This magnetic field creates intense radiation belts and auroras near its poles. Jupiter has 92 known moons, including the four largest, called the Galilean moons (Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto). Each of these moons has unique features, such as volcanic activity on Io and a possible subsurface ocean on Europa, making them of great interest to scientists. While not as prominent as Saturn's, Jupiter has a faint ring system made of dust particles. Jupiter's atmosphere is made up of hydrogen and helium, with traces of methane, ammonia, water vapor, and other compounds. The colorful bands and clouds visible on the planet are formed by its fast rotation and the mixing of gases. Jupiter rotates exremely fast, completing a full rotation in just about 10 hours, which creates a strong equatorial bulge. This fast rotation also contributes to the planet's storng weather systems and intense storms.
